Reference & Instructions

How to Use
  1. Open LeStar Z's Aimer on your phone.
  2. Place the phone on or near the playing surface where you can see it easily.
  3. Set up a cue ball and an object ball on the table. Start with an easy shot.
  4. Note the position of the object ball. Pick it up, and place the phone where it was, with the top graphic over that spot.
  5. Make sure the phone is lined up with the line to the cue ball.
  6. Move the Cut Angle slider until the arrow in the upper graphic points to the pocket or other target.
  7. Pick up the phone and put the object ball back on its spot. Then study the lower graphic — it shows you exactly where the inside edge of the cue ball needs to be placed against the object ball to produce that angle.
  8. Step up to the shot and align the real cue ball and real object ball the same way the lower graphic shows.
  9. Shoot that same setup many times until you build a feel for what that alignment looks like on the table and what it produces.
  10. Only then begin adding the lower sliders — Speed, Spin, and Distance — one at a time, then in combinations. Go slowly. Each one shifts your aim point, and you want to feel what each one does before combining them. If you are consistently missing on one side or the other, you may want to put a compensation into your estimate of the correct angle through using the Personal Bias slider.
The Two Graphics

Bird's Eye (top) — looking straight down at the table. The red object ball sits fixed on the left; the white cue ball moves to show your contact point as you adjust the cut angle. The arrow shows where the object ball will travel after contact.

Eye Level (bottom) — your view from behind the cue ball, chin near the felt. Line A runs across the face of the object ball. The tick marks divide it into sixteenths. The orange dashed line marks exactly where the inside edge of the cue ball must be aimed. The percentage badge shows that position as a fraction of the ball's width.

The Sliders

Cut Angle — the direction you want the object ball to travel. Start here. 0° is straight in, 90° is a near-miss. The aim point shown is pure geometry — everything else adjusts on top of it.

Speed — how hard you hit the shot. Slower shots give friction more time to throw the ball off the geometric line. Fast shots reduce throw. Toggle OFF to see what speed alone is contributing.

Spin — where the tip contacts the cue ball: left (inside) or right (outside). Side english adds its own throw and also causes squirt. Toggle OFF to isolate its effect.

Distance — how far the cue ball travels to reach the object ball. Squirt from english accumulates over distance. A half-tip barely matters at 6 inches but is significant at 6 feet. Toggle OFF to ignore it.

Personal Bias — a correction for your own consistent error. If you find that following the app's aim point always leaves you undercutting the shot, slide Bias toward thinner to add degrees to your aim. If you overcut, slide toward fuller. Start with small adjustments — 2° to 4° — and test on a straight-in shot first, then on cut shots. The goal is not to rely on this forever: use it to build a feel for what the correct contact looks like, then reduce it as your eye improves.

Definitions

Cut Angle — the angle between the cue ball's path and the line connecting the two ball centers at contact. 0° is straight in. 30° sends the object ball roughly 30° off to the side.

Throw — friction between the two balls at contact deflects the object ball slightly away from the pure geometric line. It always occurs on cut shots. Slower speed and outside english increase it.

Squirt — when side english is applied, the cue tip pushes the cue ball slightly sideways off its intended path at the moment of contact. The effect grows with distance traveled.

Spin / English — side rotation applied to the cue ball by striking left or right of center. Inside english means striking toward the direction of the cut. Outside english means striking away from it.

All values are approximations based on standard ball friction coefficients and a mid-weight playing cue.
Table conditions, tip hardness, and cue weight will affect results.
